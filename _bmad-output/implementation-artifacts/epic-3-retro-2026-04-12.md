## What Went Well

### New capability boundaries were added cleanly
Alarm and playlist lifecycle support landed without breaking the established `tools -> services -> adapters` architecture. `AlarmService` and `PlaylistService` became real capability boundaries instead of bolting more behavior onto unrelated modules.

### Scope hardening worked
The team did not paper over the playlist rename gap. Once review and technical research confirmed the current `SoCo` stack could not support rename cleanly, the public MCP surface was corrected to keep the contract honest.

### Backward compatibility held
Story 3.3 proved that lifecycle-created and lifecycle-updated playlists still work through the legacy `play_playlist(room, uri)` path. That is the right result: new capability, no regression in established usage.

### Test and docs consolidation happened inside the epic
Story 3.4 locked the final lifecycle contract in tests and updated examples so alarm lifecycle flows and playlist management versus playback are clearly separated. The epic did not leave docs cleanup to a future phase.

### Error-shape discipline remained strong
Epic 3 kept typed validation and unsupported-operation behavior at the service and adapter boundaries. Unsupported mutations were surfaced explicitly instead of leaking raw dependency failures.

---

## What Didn't Go Well

### Story scope and dependency reality diverged in 3.2
The biggest miss in the epic was planning accuracy, not implementation quality. Story 3.2 originally implied playlist rename support even though the chosen integration stack did not provide a supported path. Review and research corrected it, but the mismatch should have been caught before the story was treated as delivered scope.

### Tracker and artifact correction lagged behind the technical decision
Implementation removed public rename exposure, but the sprint tracker and epic/story framing still implied rename was part of active scope. That left the epic looking incomplete until the tracker was corrected today.

### Review follow-ups were still needed for lifecycle edge cases
Epic 3 required follow-up fixes around alarm discovery semantics, alarm-id validation, delete/playback interplay, and lifecycle tool verification through the public surface. These were resolved, but they indicate some acceptance criteria were not fully locked at first pass.

---

## Lessons Learned

1. **Third-party capability verification must happen before a mutation promise becomes story scope.** If a story depends on a library-backed action such as rename, the supported API path needs to be verified before the story text and acceptance criteria harden.

2. **Contract honesty is more important than nominal CRUD completeness.** The correct outcome was to ship supported create/update/delete flows and explicitly defer rename, not expose a tool that predictably fails.

3. **Scope corrections need an immediate tracked home.** Once a capability is deferred, the tracker, epic state, and deferred-work list need to change together. Otherwise completed work looks artificially unfinished.

4. **Compatibility stories add real value after a boundary split.** Story 3.3 was not redundant. It validated that adding lifecycle ownership to `PlaylistService` did not destabilize existing playback behavior.

5. **Docs and contract tests are the enforcement layer for deferred capabilities.** Story 3.4’s explicit checks that `rename_playlist` stays absent are exactly the kind of guardrail that prevents accidental contract drift later.
